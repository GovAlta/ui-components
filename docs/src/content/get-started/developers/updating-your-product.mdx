<goa-text size="heading-xl" mt="none" mb="m">Updating your product</goa-text>
<goa-text size="body-l" mt="none" mb="m">The updated design system includes a new design language, updated design tokens and components, and new templates. For your product, updating means:</goa-text>
<ul>
  <li>Your service stays aligned with the current GoA standard as more services update and users move between them</li>
  <li>You get access to new component properties, examples, and templates that reduce build time for common service types</li>
  <li>Your product remains supported — the legacy design system stops receiving fixes after September 2026</li>
</ul>
<p>
  Most of the update applies automatically. Most of the work will be reviewing what changed in
  your product and deciding where design follow-up is needed. These steps help you work through
  the process.
</p>

<goa-text as="h2" id="developer-first-approach" size="heading-m">Use the developer-first approach</goa-text>
<p>
  The developer-first approach lets you see what actually changed in your product before
  committing to design work.
</p>
<p>
  When you run the update first, spacing, typography, and token changes apply automatically. You
  can then review the result with your team and focus design and development effort on the screens
  that need it most. This avoids redesigning screens based on assumptions about what will change
  before you update.
</p>

<goa-text as="h2" id="migration-walkthrough" size="heading-m">Migration walkthrough</goa-text>
<p>
  The migration walkthrough steps through four stages — legacy design system, upgrade applied,
  layout and styling fixes, and custom component updates — and shows the visual impact on your
  interface at each stage. The walkthrough covers both public form and workspace product types.
  Review it with your team before you start: it helps designers and developers see where changes
  occur, and gives product owners a clearer picture of the scope for planning.
</p>

<goa-text as="h2" id="update-all-at-once" size="heading-m">Why we recommend updating all at once</goa-text>
<p>
  Applying the update to some parts of your product but not others tends to cause the update to
  stall. Teams focus on a few components then deprioritize the rest, leaving the product in a
  mixed state that becomes harder to resolve over time. Reviewing your updated application
  together in step 3 is designed to address this. Reviewing the full update in a branch before
  committing gives your team a clear picture of the actual work involved. Use that branch to plan
  your migration window — you are not committing to proceeding until you are ready.
</p>

<goa-text as="h2" id="migration-steps" size="heading-m">Migration steps</goa-text>

<goa-text as="h3" id="step-1" size="heading-s">Step 1: Create a branch</goa-text>
<p>
  Create a dedicated branch for the update. Keeping it separate from your regular development
  work lets your team review the changes before merging.
</p>
<pre><code>git checkout -b ds-migration</code></pre>
<p>Use a branch name that works for your team — this is just an example.</p>

<goa-text as="h3" id="step-2" size="heading-s">Step 2: Run the version update</goa-text>
<p>
  Follow the steps in <a href={withBase(`/get-started/developers/setup`)}>Setup</a> to install the
  updated packages. Run your application after installing the updates. Do this before making any
  other changes.
</p>

<goa-text as="h3" id="step-3" size="heading-s">Step 3: Review the updated application as a team</goa-text>
<p>
  Walk through your application with your product team. At this stage, the team will identify
  what needs attention and decide what to prioritize.
</p>
<p><strong>What to look for:</strong></p>

<goa-table version="2" width="100%" mb="xl">
  <table width="100%">
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Area</th>
        <th>What to look for</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td>Spacing and layout</td>
        <td>Screens that feel too tight or unbalanced</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>Typography</td>
        <td>Headings or body text that look off in size or weight</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>Dense or data-heavy screens</td>
        <td>These carry the most risk during an update and often need the most follow-up</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>Custom components</td>
        <td>Anything built outside the design system that needs manual alignment</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</goa-table>

<p>
  Record what you find. A list of screens and issues is enough at this stage. Your designers and
  product owner can use that output to plan and prioritize work.
</p>

<goa-text as="h2" id="understanding-the-effort" size="heading-m">Understanding the effort</goa-text>
<p>
  There is no single estimate for how long a migration takes. It varies depending on your
  product. The main factors are:
</p>

<goa-table version="2" width="100%" mb="xl">
  <table width="100%">
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Factor</th>
        <th>Impact on migration effort</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td>App size</td>
        <td>More screens means more to test and review after the update</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>Design system utilization</td>
        <td>Products with more custom code need more manual alignment</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>Developer familiarity</td>
        <td>Developers with more design system experience move faster through the migration steps</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</goa-table>

<p>
  Use the team review of your updated application in step 3 to build a realistic estimate for
  your own product before committing to a timeline.
</p>

<goa-text as="h2" id="getting-help" size="heading-m">Getting help</goa-text>
<ul>
  <li>Bring what you find when reviewing your updated application to drop-in hours to get feedback on what to prioritize</li>
  <li>If you find a problem with the design system itself, reach out on the support channel rather than working around it</li>
</ul>
